Unit 1: Scientific Investigations and Ethics

**Unit objectives**
- Differrentiate between inductive and deductive reasoning.
- Understand why ethics are important and how they may relate to your area of research and your professional practice.

**Brief reflections from unit 1**

The module began with a review of Ethics in Computing.
- One thing to be clear from the outset, is that Ethics and Legality, are not always aligned,
  and this may lead to less than desirable situations (Resnik, 2020).
- Topics such as Data Protection Regulations, may help to protect somewhat the end users, however,
  with regulations differing somewhat between countries, and in some countries like the US, even
  within the different States themselves, there is no guarantee of this.
- People have a fundamental right to privacy, and this must be respected.
- Good research follows some guildelines and rtespects the law/s (ÖAWI, 2015).
	
	
References:
- Resnik, David B. (2020). What is Ethics in Research & Why Is It Important? Available from: 
  https://www.niehs.nih.gov/research/resources/bioethics/whatis/index.cfm
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].
- ÖAWI. (2015). OeAWI Guildelines for Good Scientific Practice. Available from: https://oeawi.at/en/guidelines/
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].

Unit 4: Case Studies, Focus Groups and Observations


**Unit objectives**
- Understand how to carry out each type of data collection methods.
- Discuss the advantage and drawback of the different methods.
- Consider the types of data you will obtain.
	
**Brief reflections from unit 4**
- Different data collection methods include Case Studies, Focus Groups, Quantitative observation, Qualitative observation.
- Advantages of focus groups is that discussion may be open-ended and lead to new avenues of research and flexibility in methods of data collection
  (UNSW, ND). Disadvantages include "following the crowd" and "groupthink" where participants do not share ideas in group setting (SIS, ND).
- Quantitative is data dealing with numbers - how much?, how many?, how often?. Qualitative is less specific. Can get a feeling for the data,
  but it is open to interpretation (Stevens, 2023).
  Benefits and drawbacks to both methodologies (GCU, 2023).
	
References:
- GCU. (2023). Qualitative vs. Quantitative Research: What's the Difference? Available from: 
  https://www.gcu.edu/blog/doctoral-journey/qualitative-vs-quantitative-research-whats-difference
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].
- SIS. (ND). Advantages of Focus Groups. Available from: https://www.sisinternational.com/advantages-of-focus-groups/#
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].
- Stevens, Emily. (2023). Quantitative vs Qualitative Data: What's the Difference? Available from: 
  https://careerfoundry.com/en/blog/data-analytics/difference-between-quantitative-and-qualitative-data/#:~:text=What%20are%20examples%20of%20qualitative,scores%2C%20and%20website%20traffic%20data.

Unit 5: Interviews and Survey Methods


**Unit objectives**
- Understand how interviews and surveys fit into research and whether either or both of these might be useful for your investigation.
- Understand how pre- and post-testing can be used in research.	
	
**Brief reflections from unit 5**
- Surveys obtain data directly from target audience, with potential to include large number of respondents (Jones et al, 2013).
- Multiple collection methods, including 1-2-1 interviews (by phone or in person), emailed questionnaire, paper copy etc,
  all with different benefits and drawbacks (Ponto, 2015).
- pre- and post-testing allows for verification after a new variable has been introduced, to see if there is a discernable change in response (Stratton, 2019).

References:
- Jones, TL. Baxter, MAJ. Khanduja,V. (2013). A quick guide to survey research.
  Available from: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3964639/#:~:text=Survey%20research%20is%20a%20unique,the%20availability%20of%20validated%20models.
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].
- Ponto, Julie. (2015). Understanding and Evaluating Survey Research. 
  Available from: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/286445115_Understanding_and_Evaluating_Survey_Research
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].
- Stratton, Samel J. (2019). Quasi-Experimental Design (Pre-Test and Post-Test Studies) in prehospital and Disaster Research.
  Available from: https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/prehospital-and-disaster-medicine/article/quasiexperimental-design-pretest-and-posttest-studies-in-prehospital-and-disaster-research/13DC743E82CE9CC6407998A05C6E1560

Unit 6: Questionnaire Design


**Unit objectives**
- Tell the difference between a good questionnaire and a poor one.
- Design a questionnaire to obtain the responses you will need for your investigation.
- Understand how the data obtained can be analysed.
	
**Brief reflections from unit 6**
- Importance of good survey/questionnaire design cannot be understated.
  Good data is critical at beginning of research phase. If bad data is analysed, it is difficult to achieve clear conclusions (Parab et al, 2010).
- Survey should be written without pre-guessing results to avoid manipulation and bias (Pannucci et al, 2010), (Charlesworth, 2022)..
	
References:
- Charlesworth. (2022). Biases at Different Stages of Research - and ways to avoid them. 
  Available from: https://www.cwauthors.com/article/avoiding-biases-at-different-stages-of-research
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].
- Pannucci, Christopher J. Wilkins, Edwin G. (2010). Identifying and Avoiding Bias in Research. 
  Available from: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2917255/
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].
- Parab, Shraddha. Bhalerao, Supriya. (2010). Study Designs. Available from: 
  https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2924977/#:~:text=The%20design%20of%20any%20study,generated%20can%20be%20best%20analyzed.
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].

Unit 7: Validity and Generalisability in Research


**Unit objectives**
- Understand how the concepts of validity, generalisability and reliability affect your investigation and 
   the design of your research method.
- Consider how to analyse and present the results you obtain from your investigation and how they will
   enable you to answer your research question.

**Brief reflections from unit 7**	
- Presentation of results from investigation, need to be clear and concise.
- Validity is extent to which the data achieved what it set out to achieve
  Reliability is the consistency of the data collected - can it be trusted?
  Generalisability is the extent to which the findings can be applied in other settings (Shantikumar, 2018).
- Where possible, any findings or conclusions, under right conditions, may be repeatable.
- Questionnaire must be fit for purpose, and address through the questions, the topic that it is targetting.

	
References:
- Shantikumar, Saran. (2018). Validdity, reliability and generalisability. 
  Available from: https://www.healthknowledge.org.uk/content/validity-reliability-and-generalisability
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].

Unit 8: Inferential Statistics


**Unit objectives**
- Identify the different levels of measurement.
- Produce measures of location and spread.
- Perform appropriate hypothesis tests.

**Brief reflections from unit 8**	
- Statistics can still be used to prove anything you want, however, they are a useful tool to analyse data (Bhandari, 2023).
- Importance of source data cannot be understated. If it comes from questionnaires, questions should be intelligently designed.
- Statistics are the best way to reject or accept hypotheses (Cuemath, ND), (Aransiola, 2021).
- Statistics allow us to visualise with numbers, the spread of results obtained through the data (Humansofdata, 2018).

References:

  Available from: https://www.formpl.us/blog/inferential-statistics-definition-types-examples
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].

  [Accessed 29 October 2023].

Unit 10: Research Writing


**Unit objectives**
- Understand how to structure a dissertation and how to approach each of them.
	
**Brief reflections from unit 10**
- Planning is essential.
  Must be organised.
- Writing must be performed often - no possibility of completing a project "at the last minute".
- It should follow a well established presentation template (MIT, ND).
- Where possible, it should be on a subject that the author/s find interesting, as it will occupy a lot of their time.


References:
- Farquhar, Jillian Dawes. (2012). Writing and Presenting Your Research. 
  Available from: https://methods-sagepub-com.uniessexlib.idm.oclc.org/book/case-study-research-for-business/n9.xml
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].
- MIT. (ND). Format of the Thesis. Available from: https://www.mit.edu/course/21/21.guide/th-form.htm
  [Accessed 29 October 2023].
