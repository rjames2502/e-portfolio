Security and Risk Management 

This module will focus on the increasing requirement to pair Security and risk together in modern business. 
The module will cover a broad range of THREAT Models that are available, qualitative and quantitative assessments, 
as well as describing how to perform and carry out appropriate assessments.
As part of this module, it is required to work in small groups, in order to perform assessments of a business
and the risks associated with digitization and additionally becoming an online business.

The module is run over 12 weeks, split into 12 units.

Personal REFLECTIONS of the module will be recorded at the end of this page
							
Unit 1: An Introduction to Security and Risk Management
							
"Risk management is the process of identifying risk, assessing risk, and taking steps to reduce risk to an acceptable level". 
The module begins by explaining why risk needs to be continually assessed, and how these assessments can help tailor the defences
appropriately to protect an Enterprise.

It can be split into 3 parts - 
1) Determine the different risks and vulnerabilities that may be presented, and the likelihood of them occurring.
2) Evaluate and prioritize the risk and establish mitigation processes/techniques against them.
3) Continual evaluation and assessment, so as things change, the mitigation can be adapted.

The unit continues to describe different methodologies for risk assessment, and shows where it can sometimes
go wrong during assessment. 
The favoured method uses quantitive analysis methods, as opposed to qualititive, as they are less subjective
and should be more accurate to define.

Industry 4.0 is introduced, as a method to digitize traditional manufacturing processe in order to make them more efficient.

Unit 5: An Introduction to Security and Risk Standards in Industry and the Enterprise


During this unit, we covered the different types of data standards that have to be adhered to. GDPR, ISO27000, PCIO-DSS and 
NIST were covered.There was a comprehensive coverage of how to incorporate adherence to these Data Standards into organisations
successfully. Having a defined standard in place, but not confirming to it, could be both financially and reputationally damaging
for a company.
Different indistries have different requirements, and from a business perspective, it is important to know which apply, 
especially as in many instances, more than one standard is valid.
During the group seminar, I discussed a Case Study called "Right to be Forgotten" from https://dataprotection.ie/en/pre-gdpr/case-studies#201701
In this study, it described a case where a person was effectively destroyed online, through information pertaining to them partaking in criminal
activities, and losing their job due to poor performance and dishonesty.
This information was widely published, leading to the persons reputation being damaged extensively.
The truth however, was far different. 
The person had been found "not guilty" in a court of law, and had actually left their job voluntarily for personal reasons, and on good terms.
The discussion was primarily about the complete lack of control from the search engine, and a complete disregard for personal data privacy.
The search engine stated, that it was in the public interest to publish this story, but they had not performed due dilligence to verify that the 
information was correct and accurate.
Once the information is published, it is very difficult to ensure that all erronous data is removed from every site on the Internet.
Subsequent searches, will likely find reference to false information.
We discussed how it would be prudent to implement strong fines for false data release, however, these are probably quite difficult
to implement globally.

Unit 7: An Introduction to the Concepts of Quantitive Risk Modelling


This week focused on 2 main quantitiative risk assessment models - Monte Carlo and Bayes Theorem.
Multiple Crisis Decision Analysis (MCDA) is also discussed, and like all methods, requires appropriate selection of data for
analysis.
TOPSIS, AHP and ANP are discussed, and give some feedback about which method is most appropriate to be used, depending
on the required output.
These methodologies are designed to be repeatable and not open to personal interpretation, meaning that it should be possible
to run the algorithms multiple times, by multiple unconnected people, yet still achieve the same results each time.
These methodoligies however, are very complex in comparison to other qualitative methods such as CVSS.

Unit 9: Risk, Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery


Disaster recovery was introduced this week, as a way of mitigating some risk.
DR can cover different many scenarios starting from having good backups of data that can be restored, through to Enterprise level requirements.
At the top end of the scale, DR can consist of multiple Datacenters in the Cloud or geographically seperately located, with continuous replication between 
them, of all data and systems.
Risk analysis is relevant to DR, as it is important to understand what the impact to a business is, if their data is unavailable. The length 
of time that the business can run without this access, helps to dictate the level of DR preparedness that is implemented.
Naturally, cost vs risk needs to also be considered.
Concepts such as RTO (Recovery Time Objective) and RPO (Recovery Point Objective) were also introduced, as a way to determine the level of DR required.						

Unit 10: Practical Applications and Issues in DR Implementations

Disaster recovery continued this week, and focused more on the Cloud options available.
Cloud is the buzzword at the moment, and is heralded as the answer to a lot of problems.
Whilst it has its benefits and advantages, there are definately some drawbacks that are associated 
with this model - security, cost, vendor lock-in and lack of control are most prominent.
Mitigation is possible to reduce the negative aspects, but there is always a balance that needs to
be weighed.

As part of the seminar preparation, we were asked to detail issues with both vendor lock-in when migrating to the cloud, as well as any security
concerns with the cloud itself.
The issues with vendor lock-in, are caused by different CSPs using proprietary software or formats. This makes it very difficult for any user/company
to migrate their data away in the future, either to premises or another CSP.
The best way to alleviate this, is to insist that the data is stored in Open or common standards. This way, any tie-in of the data itself will be 
removed.
Regarding security in the cloud, a lot of the problem is having to trust a CSP to secure the enviromnet properly.
Having agreed standards with the CSP before deployment in the cloud, and periodic checks and verifications can help to alleviate this risk as well.

Unit 11: Futute Trends in Security and Risk Management


We started to look at future trends in the Security and Risk Management area.
Changes in Enterprise Risk Management were explored, as well as looking at new methodologies for SRM assessments.
Also covered was Development Security Operations (DevSecOps), which is essentially calling for tighter cooperation 
between Programmers, System Administrators and Security experts, throughout the complete software delivery lifecycle.
This extended cooperation could help to ensure that security aspects are integrated completely into the developement process, and thus
ensuring more secure and stable releases of code.
Artifical Intelligence(AI)/Machine Learning(ML) was also introduced.
AI can help to automate defence from attacks, as well as aid in. keeping systems patched and upto date with the latest code or security revisions.
